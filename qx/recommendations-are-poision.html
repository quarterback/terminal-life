You Are Who You Recommend

Originally published in 2017

Social media recommendations make intuitive sense. New user? Give them someone to follow. From the beginning, platforms like Twitter offered you a list of famous, verified people. LinkedIn evolved into an influencer showcase. The idea: if people see successful figures, they’ll want to stick around.

But this mechanism is not neutral. It imposes a value structure before you’ve made a choice. These systems reward broadcast-style behavior, reinforcing voices that already have reach and power. It’s not about connecting peers—it’s about shaping what "value" looks like on the platform.

The perverse incentives scale down. Small networks mimic this design logic and begin incentivizing thinkfluencer behavior. The veneer of connection is performative.

Recommender systems embedded in UX flows frame the user’s needs before they’re even expressed. That framing encodes harm. It presumes what a user should want, and then subtly enforces it through nudges, defaults, and platform tone.

This isn’t about optimizing for discovery—it’s about reinforcing hierarchy. Recommendations become assertions of worthiness. And in doing so, they poison the space for emergent voices to find footing on their own terms.
