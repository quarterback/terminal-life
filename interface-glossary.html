Interface Discourse Traps: A Taxonomy

Compiled as part of the parasitarchy framework

Not all traps are lies. Some are architectures. Below is a working taxonomy of recurring interface discourse traps — rhetorical sleights, conceptual shortcuts, and ambient conditions — that the parasitarchy framework identifies and critiques.

What is a discourse trap?
A recurring pattern that limits how we can talk about power, governance, or systems — often by framing problems as neutral, technical, or inevitable.
  • Friction Aversion: Assuming that removing friction (bureaucratic, social, procedural) is always progress. This trap masks where friction protects rights, deliberation, or care work.
  • Abundance Shortcut: Framing material problems as purely supply-side, while ignoring the social conditions that shape distribution, access, and trust.
  • Administrative Aesthetics: Confusing interface polish or technical coherence for institutional competence.
  • Post-Political Drift: Framing political or structural conflicts as engineering challenges. Often seen in tech-forward policy discourse where disagreement is recast as design inefficiency.
  • Manageable Crisis: Treating systemic breakdowns as isolated anomalies solvable through better tooling, instead of recognizing their structural causes.
  • Retroactive Consent: Using engagement metrics or interface clicks as evidence of public will, flattening collective intent into behavioral data.
  • Interface Realism: Treating digital affordances as metaphysically primary — as if a platform's design defines the limits of the real, the possible, the political.

This taxonomy is not static. If anything, the traps evolve with the systems they cloak. But naming them is a start — a way to loosen their grip on our thought and build room for deeper critique.
