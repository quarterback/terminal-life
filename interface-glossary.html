Interface Glossary

A living vocabulary from the Consequence Design Reader

This glossary catalogs the recurring traps, metaphors, and rhetorical sleights that shape how we talk about digital systems. These terms aren’t neutral—they define what counts as legitimate critique, what gets dismissed as inefficiency, and what disappears entirely.

Not all traps are lies. Some are architectures.

The list below is part taxonomy, part diagnosis. It emerged through the development of the Consequence Design framework and the broader parasitarchy thesis. These traps evolve. But naming them gives us a way to think alongside their contours instead of inside their constraints.

Discourse Trap: A recurring rhetorical or structural pattern that limits how systems, institutions, or interfaces are allowed to be criticized — often by reducing political concerns to technical ones.
  • Friction Aversion: The belief that removing friction is inherently good. Ignores how friction protects process, dignity, or care.
  • Abundance Shortcut: Treating supply as the solution to all distribution problems. Depoliticizes scarcity and erases systemic exclusion.
  • Administrative Aesthetics: Mistaking visual polish or interface flow for institutional legitimacy.
  • Post-Political Drift: Recasting political disagreement as technical inefficiency. Often deployed in “smart governance” schemes.
  • Manageable Crisis: Treating institutional collapse as a tooling issue rather than a structural inevitability.
  • Retroactive Consent: Using interface clicks or behavior logs to justify a lack of deliberation or opt-out pathways.
  • Interface Realism: Believing that what can be done through the interface *is* the real—flattening politics into platform affordances.

This glossary will evolve. Contributions, critiques, or new trap sightings are welcome.
